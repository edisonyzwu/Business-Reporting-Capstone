Manufactured Homes Seen Easing Housing Crisis as Builders Slump

By Edison Wu

May 5, 2025

While high mortgage rates and Trump tariffs continue to weigh on the US home construction industry, manufactured housing companies are thriving against the backdrop.

Cavco Industries Inc., a Phoenix, Arizona-based manufactured home company, climbed 30% in the past 12 months, the biggest increase among all home builders, according to data compiled by Bloomberg.

Rivals Bedford, Texas-based Legacy Housing Corp. and Troy, Michigan-based Champion Homes Inc. gained 18% and 8.7%, respectively, while the broader home construction sector fell 14%, suggesting growing investor interest in factory-built homes as an alternative to traditional housing.

Cavco says it is expanding production to meet rising demand. “If we had a dollar to spend, we probably would spend that dollar in growing our existing plant capacity,” Chief Financial Officer Allison K. Aden said during a Jan. 31 earnings call.

Housing Affordability Constraints

The surge in manufactured housing comes as US housing affordability continues to hover near historically low levels. The National Association of Home Builders, based in Washington, DC, says the median price of a traditional single-family home climbs to $400,000. Nearly 60% of American households can’t afford a $300,000 home, according to Bloomberg data.

The US economy shrank in early 2025, the first contraction since 2022, on a monumental pre-tariffs import surge and more moderate consumer spending. Many executives cited the collapse in consumer confidence and the potential for a more guarded approach to spending.

Paul J. Romanowski, chief executive officer of D.R. Horton Inc., said during an April 17 earnings call that the housing market has softened this year. The Arlington-based company’s shares have dropped 34% since a record high in September 2024.

“This year’s spring selling season started slower than expected as potential homebuyers have been more cautious due to continued affordability constraints and declining consumer confidence,” Romanowski said.

Factory-Built Alternatives

Manufactured homes have been seen as an alternative at lower prices. The average sales price of a new factory-built home was about $127,300 in 2023, less than a third of the cost of a site-built home, according to the US Census Bureau.

“Our target market of homebuyers consists of households with total annual income below $75,000, which comprised 47% of total US households in 2023,” said Duncan Bates, chief executive officer of Texas-based Legacy Housing during a March 12 earnings call, “This group, nearly half of all households in the United States, has been severely impacted by increasing rental rates, higher prices for site-built homes, elevated mortgage rates, and stagnant wage growth.”

Factory-built homes are also gaining traction as disaster recovery solutions. Billionaire Joe Gebbia, co-founder of San Francisco, California-based Airbnb Inc. and board member of Austin, Texas-based Tesla Inc., is donating $15 million of pre-fabricated homes through his startup Samara to low-income residents whose properties burned in the Los Angeles wildfires. The two-bedroom, two-bath units can be built in as little as five months and installed within weeks.

History of Pre-fabricated Homes

Prefabricated homes trace back to 1908, when Illinois-based Sears, Roebuck & Co., sold 70,000 mail-order houses. Buyers received materials and could either build the homes or hire contractors.

The industry peaked in 1972 with 600,000 units shipped, accounting for 60% of new single-family units, according to US Census data, even as mobile homes during that time faced structural and safety issues and lost value faster than site-built units.

In 1974, Congress passed the Mobile Home Safety Standards Act, establishing federal rules and enforcement. The same year, a recession triggered by the energy crisis pushed the housing market into decline. Mobile home production dropped and never returned to peak levels.

Manufactured homes made up 9% to 10% of new housing over the past decade, rising by 1 percentage point in the past year, according to the Manufactured Housing Survey.

Shifting Policy

Challenges persist. Zoning laws in many municipalities restrict where manufactured homes can be placed. Financing options remain limited. Buyers turn to personal property loans, which carry higher rates than traditional mortgages, because most manufactured homes sit on leased land and aren’t classified as real estate, making them ineligible for conventional home loans.

Industry groups are lobbying for regulatory reforms, seeking changes to zoning rules and expanded access to standard mortgage financing to unlock demand.

Federal and state policies are shifting. In September 2024, the US Department of Housing and Urban Development announced the most extensive revisions to the manufactured home construction and safety standards in more than 30 years. The changes, covering 90 new or amended standards, allow for the construction of multi-unit manufactured homes such as duplexes and triplexes for the first time.

In California, state law requires cities and counties to permit federally certified manufactured homes on lots zoned for single-family residences under Government Code Section 65852.3. The law mandates that manufactured homes meet the same development standards as site-built homes.

The implications are growing. With traditional homebuilders slowing output and affordability challenges persisting, manufactured housing offers a potential solution to the US housing shortage.

Market Expansion

While manufactured housing remains a slice with 6% of US home stock, it is spread across states, with concentration in the rural areas of the South and East, according to Census Bureau data. Industry players see room for expansion as new housing policies and economic pressures reshape the housing landscape.

“I think where affordability is the worst, that's where municipalities are challenging their thinking and trying to figure out how to open up to really a better option for housing and more close in and urban areas, which is factory-built housing,” said William C. Boor, chief executive officer of Cavco, “So, I feel good about the directional trend and it's always just been a question of how fast and when.”
